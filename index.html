Research

Mitosis is a highly preserved process in all eukaryotic cells. A crucial step during chromosomes segregation in the transition between metaphase to anaphase, where sister chromosomes segregate and migrate to their respective cell poles. The actors of the mitotic machinary are well known: it includes microtubules (Mts), kinetochores (Kts) – which are assemblies of multiples proteins connecting mechanically Mts to the chromosomes – and their respective regulators. Neverthless, the way these proteins interact in space and time to produce a proper attachment and pulling on chromosomes is still poorly understood.

Schizosaccharomyces pombe (S. pombe) is used as a model system to understand the dynamics of the mitotic spindle. With only three chromosomes and four Mts connected to each Kt, S. pombe is the perfect candidate to give quantitative insights into chromosome movements during mitosis. During metaphase, the chromosomes of S. pombe – like in most of the eukaryotic species – exhibit oscillations along the mitotic spindle, before to align at the cell center prior to anaphase.

In order to define and characterize these events, we use a robust analysis framework - flow from video microscopy acquisition to chromosome trajectory analysis. We are implementing tools such as peak detection algorithm and robust single particle tracker. Most of the features of these trajectories found in vivo has been reproduced in silico with a simple mechanical model of the mitotic spindle (Gay et al., 2012).
